★ START ★
The Concept

We started by brainstorming how users would interact with the game — researching carnival games, mechanics for winning prizes, games designed to be difficult to win, single-round games, and competitive multiplayer games.

We considered what the win/lose dynamic would look like. Since the core concept was simple, we looked for ways to add difficulty and entertainment.

Requirements: fun, playable solo or as a team, carry a meaningful message, easy to explain, and high engagement.

★ LOW FI ★
Cardboard Prototype

Our prototype consisted of a 60×60 cm maze with 5 cm tall walls built from cardboard — giving a much clearer idea of what the project would look like physically, how we wanted it to feel, and what the user experience would be like.

After presenting to our tutors, we identified key improvements: a shock delivery element, a countdown clock for time pressure, and reconsidering the mechanism responsible for moving the maze (arms, base, or moving frame).

★ DESIGN DEVELOP ★
Refinements

We improved interaction point placement (shocks and portals), planned a display base for the exhibition, and thought through materials needed for the final model. Size, structure, and strategy all evolved here.

We also re-evaluated the mechanism for moving the maze — settling on a servo-driven arm system that was rigid enough for gameplay but precise enough for control.

★ 3D MODELLING ★
Digital Model

The 3D model confirmed exact dimensions and visualised how all components would sit together — the acrylic base layers, wall pieces, servo mounts, and the overhead drop-ship assembly.

Slotted joints (teeth on walls, slots in base) were designed here — locking pieces together without glue for better resistance and easy disassembly between sessions.

★ FEATURES ★

Throughout the semester, every feature went through multiple evolution cycles — driven by problem-solving, component compatibility, and improving the interaction between players and the maze.

FEATURE 01
⚡ Shock Effect

Originally, sensors in the maze would connect to a shock station. After testing we found it wasn't viable — pivoted to a small vibration effect instead.

FEATURE 02
🤝 Contact to Move

Players complete a circuit by holding hands — sending a signal to a servo motor to tilt the maze. Iterated through gloves → coins → foil knuckle bands.

★ FINAL DESIGN ★
Complete System

For the final stretch, we had achieved a great part of our initial concept. We successfully adapted to challenges, overcame setbacks, and by the mid-file milestone delivered a fully functional maze.

The full system — movement, sensing, ball drop, and vibration feedback — worked together as one coherent interactive experience with a polished exhibition-ready enclosure.

Public Play

The project was exhibited publicly, where visitors played the maze cooperatively — connecting hands to move the board and race against the clock. The response validated our core design decisions: approachable, social, and genuinely hard to win.
